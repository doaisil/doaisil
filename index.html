The Digital Product Passport (DPP) Era is Here

From Directive to Regulation: A Legally Binding Mandate

As of August 18, 2025, the EU's new Battery Regulation is fully in force, officially replacing the previous directive. This is a critical legal distinction: directives require member states to enact their own laws, while regulations are directly and uniformly applicable across all 27 EU countries. This act makes the Digital Product Passport a mandatory, non-negotiable requirement for batteries placed on the EU market. The DPP must be complete, accurate, and continuously updated throughout the product's lifecycle, creating an immediate and urgent need for a robust software solution to manage this data.

A Greenfield Market for Compliance-as-a-Service

The DPP mandate for batteries is the first wave of a much broader regulatory push under the Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (ESPR). Similar DPP requirements are already in the pipeline for textiles, electronics, and construction materials. This creates a massive, greenfield market opportunity for a "Compliance-as-a-Service" platform. Companies will need a scalable solution to collect, manage, and share granular product-level data across their entire supply chain. This is no longer about operational efficiency; it is about the legal right to sell products in one of the world's largest markets.

International Partnerships and EPR-Driven Funding

Global Knowledge Transfer Creates New Markets

The formal collaboration between Istanbul and Jakarta on waste management, including landfill operations, compost production, and carbon credits, is a strong indicator of a global trend. As emerging economies seek to modernize their infrastructure, they are looking to established players for proven technologies and best practices. This creates opportunities for software providers like Evreka to enter new markets as part of these knowledge-sharing agreements, offering a ready-made digital foundation for these modernization efforts.

EPR Continues to Drive Capital Flow

In California, brands are now directly offering funding to recyclers to improve the collection of film and flexible packaging. This is a direct result of the state's EPR law (SB 54). This trend shows how capital is flowing from producers to recyclers to build the infrastructure necessary to meet compliance targets. This creates a need for software that can manage these financial flows, track material recovery rates, and provide auditable data back to the producers to prove their EPR obligations are being met.
